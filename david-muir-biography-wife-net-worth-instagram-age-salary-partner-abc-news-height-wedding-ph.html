Biography

David Jason Muir (born November 8, 1973) is a distinguished American journalist. He commands the anchor desk for ABC World News Tonight and serves as the co-anchor of the esteemed ABC News magazine 20/20. Additionally, he fulfills the crucial role of primary substitute anchor for the acclaimed ABC program Good Morning America.

Born in Syracuse, New York, David Muir‘s passion for journalism bloomed early on. He cultivated his skills at the renowned Park School of Communications, graduating in 1995 with an education that would become the bedrock of his illustrious career.

David Muir‘s journey into journalism began in his hometown of Syracuse, where he cut his teeth as a reporter and weekend anchor at WTVH-TV. Eager to broaden his horizons, he embarked on a new chapter at WCVB-TV in Manchester, New Hampshire, showcasing his talents as a reporter and anchor.

In 2003, David Muir joined the esteemed ranks of ABC News, making his mark as a New York-based correspondent. He quickly established himself as a trusted voice in the field, which led to his appointment as the news anchor for Good Morning America Weekend Edition and the Weekend Edition of World News Tonight.

Early Life & Education

David Muir, the renowned news anchor, was born on November 8, 1973, in Syracuse, New York. Raised in the embrace of Onondaga Hill, he found comfort and camaraderie within the loving confines of his Catholic family. 

Alongside his older brother, two stepbrothers, and a constellation of six nieces and three nephews, David‘s journey was colored by the bonds of kinship. A cherished sister named Rebecca Muir completed the tapestry of his familial connections. Together, they shared moments of laughter, growth, and unconditional support with their parents, Ronald Muir and Pat Mills.

From a tender age, David Muir was captivated by the power of news. As evening fell, the household would gather around the television, their eyes fixed on the main program of ABC News. During these shared experiences, the seeds of inspiration were sown, with veteran anchor Peter Jennings serving as David‘s beacon of journalistic excellence. The resonant voice and commanding presence of Jennings left an indelible mark on the young mind, fueling his desire to pursue a career in news reporting.

With his high school years closing, David Muir embarked on a new chapter at Onondaga Central Junior-Senior High School. After graduating in May 1991, he set his sights on Ithaca College, a prestigious institution renowned for its Park School of Communications. There, he delved into the art of journalism, immersing himself in a curriculum designed to sharpen his skills and ignite his passion for storytelling. In May 1995, with a Bachelor of Arts degree in journalism, Muir emerged from his academic cocoon, ready to take flight.

Yet, even during his formative years, David Muir understood the value of hands-on experience. Throughout high school, he embarked on an internship at WTVH-TV in Syracuse, eagerly absorbing the intricacies of the broadcasting world. These early encounters shaped his perspective and provided him with a glimpse into the dynamic nature of news reporting.

While the halls of academia paved his path, the serendipitous encounters truly moulded David Muir‘s destiny. A perceptive lecturer recognized the spark within him, declaring that he possessed the essence of a TV newsman. Encouraged by these words, Muir sought to broaden his horizons. He seized the opportunity to participate in Georgetown University‘s Fund for American Studies Institute on Political Journalism, exploring the nuances of political reporting. Further expanding his worldview, he embarked on a transformative semester abroad at the University of Salamanca in Spain, immersing himself in a diverse cultural landscape and honing his understanding of global affairs.

Career

David Muir was an anchor and reporter at WTVH-TV in Syracuse, New York, from 1994 to 2000. Following the killing of Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin in 1995, Muir‘s reporting from Jerusalem, Tel Aviv, Israel, and the Gaza Strip earned him top prizes from the Radio-Television News Directors Association.

He received awards from the Associated Press for Best Enterprise Reporting and Best Television Interview. The Syracuse Press Club named Muir the “Best Local Newscast” anchor, and he was named one of Syracuse’s “Best Local News Anchors.” He worked as an anchor and reporter for WCVB in Boston from 2000 to 2003, when he won the regional Edward R. Murrow Award for investigative reporting as well as the National Headliner Award.

He was also recognized by the Associated Press for his work in tracing the journey of the hijackers engaged in the September 11, 2001, assaults. His news anchoring and reporting were also acknowledged by the Associated Press. David Muir joined ABC News in August 2003 as the overnight news program World News Now anchor. He also became the anchor of ABC News’ World News This Morning in the early morning (America This Morning). Muir began anchoring World News Saturday in June 2007.

He co-anchored the news magazine Primetime in 2006 and on occasion after that. David Muir took over as weekend news anchor in February 2012, and the show was renamed World News with David Muir. He has been discreetly credited with increasing Sunday evening broadcast ratings. He was promoted to co-anchor ABC’s 20/20 with Elizabeth Vargas in March 2013.

David Muir was one of ABC’s top correspondents for the 2012 U.S. Presidential election in November 2012. His interviews with Republican nominee Mitt Romney sparked a national debate in the United States about economics and immigration policy. His Emmy-nominated Made In America series on the American economy is still running on his show. He has brought the show to other television shows, such as ABC’s The View, where he has been a guest co-host.

David Muir reported from inside Iran in the run-up to the nuclear talks in January 2013. He was the first Western journalist to report on the famine from Mogadishu, Somalia. While reporting from Mogadishu, he and his colleagues came under fire. For his reporting, he got the Edward R. Murrow Award in 2013.
