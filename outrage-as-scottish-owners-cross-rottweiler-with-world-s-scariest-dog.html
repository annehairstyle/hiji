Outrage as Scottish owners cross rottweiler with world's scariest dog

Posted by Billy Koelling on Friday, June 7, 2024

TWO Scots “backyard breeders” have left safety campaigners horrified by selling deadly cross-bred “superdogs” to families.

James Bell and Mark McCallie mated a rottweiler bitch with a powerful Presa Canario, – a breed banned in Australia and New Zealand and blamed for two deaths in America.

One UK breeder describes Presa Canarios as “the beastliest dogs in the world”.

Bell, 29, and McCallie, 24, claim their “presweiler” pups make great family pets.

But their experiment has been angrily condemned by a senior vet at an animal charity – and the mother of an 11-year-old girl killed by two rottweilers.

Kellie Lynch’s mum Veronica said: “To cross two dangerous breeds beggars belief. These men could have made a shark on land.

“Irresponsible people who experiment with crossing pure breeds should go to jail for it.”

The veterinary director at charity Dogs Trust, Paula Boyden, added: “I am speechless, to say the least. It is totally irresponsible and potentially dangerous for these people to experiment like this.

“You cannot predict what you are going to get out of it.

“I question their motivation for trying to create this dog. It could be a superdog for all the wrong reasons.”

Mechanic Bell, of Irvine, Ayrshire, and Dumfries livestock yardsman McCallie produced their presweilers by mating McCallie’s rottweiler bitch with a stud Presa Canario from England.

They had to spend months looking for a Presa Canario breeder who would let them use one of his dogs.

The pair kept two of the pups from the litter for breeding and are touting them to owners for mating with other large dogs.

The other puppies were sold to families.

McCallie and Bell admit they could be described as backyard breeders and they expect registered breeders to criticise them for what they have done.

But they insist they have no regrets.

McCallie told the Record: “Our presweilers are fantastic dogs and they are good with children. I kept one of them, called Bear, and he is great with my 16-month-old daughter.

“They have a great temperament and look stunning.

“People who breed pure-bred rottweilers and presas will probably frown on this because they want to keep everything pure.

“But by keeping everything pure, they are breeding dogs that are too closely related and causing problems.”

Bell claimed that crossing rottweilers with presas produced dogs with better temperaments and removed genetic faults such as hip problems.

He added: “We want to get the presweiler recognised as a proper breed but it could take up to 10 years.

“There has been a lot of bad publicity surrounding these dogs. But it’s the owners to blame, not the dogs.

“We will make sure our dogs don’t get into the wrong hands, to be used as status symbols or for dog fighting by the shellsuited fraternity.”

Bell has placed an advert online offering his 14-month-old presweiler, Nero, for breeding.

It says: “Nero has a wonderful temperament. He is great with children, cats and other dogs. He is also a great guard dog.”

But the advert also emphasises the dog’s size and build. It says of Nero: “He is still growing at the moment, weighing 58kg, and is very athletic and muscular.”

Bell and McCallie refused to say how much they would charge for puppies or the use of their dogs in breeding.

Pure-bred Presa Canarios can sell for up to £1000, with rottweilers fetching about £300.

Experts dismissed Bell and McCallie’s claim that crossing rottweilers and presas would produce a safer dog.

Paula Boyden said: “It’s nonsense. I can’t see the logic.

“The presa is susceptible to the same health problems as the rottweiler and both are aggressive, strong, powerful dogs renowned for guarding instincts.”

Paula claimed that “if you cross them, you may exacerbate these problems”.

“It is very worrying,” she added. “The problem is that there are no laws against it.”

Veronica Lynch, who lost her daughter in 1989 and has fought for tougher dog laws ever since, is furious that breeders can experiment with no legal checks.

She said: “It is so slack. Owners can do anything they want without a second thought for
the public.”

Christine Grahame, the SNP MSP behind Scotland’s 2010 Control of Dogs Act, said owners were legally obliged to keep dogs under control.

But Veronica dismissed the law as “useless”. She said: “We need preventative measures, like muzzling and compulsory leads when dogs are out in public.

“The law as it stands is a joke. It gives powers to the authorities to act only after an attack.”
